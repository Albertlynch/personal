What is a logical fallacy?

A logical fallacy is a flaw in reasoning or an error in the structure of an argument that makes it invalid or weak, even if the conclusion might still be true. Logical fallacies often occur in debates, discussions, or persuasive writing and can mislead or distract from the real issue.

Types of logical fallacies

Logical Fallacies are separated into two main groups: Formal Fallacies, and Informal Fallacies. A formal Fallacy is when there is a structural error in the conversation, like affirming the consequent. For example, if you think option A is wrong, they automatically assume that you agree with option B. Now, an informal fallacy is an error in reasoning based on the content or the context or the argument instead of structure. There are many different fallacies in these two main groups, and we will cover the most commonly used ones.

Formal Fallacies

Affirming the consequent

Affirming the consequent is one of the three formal fallacies. It is also known as converse error, fallacy of the converse, or confusion of necessity and sufficiency. It happens when the consequent of the conversation is true, and the person assumes that the antecedent is automatically true.

Denying the Antecedent

Denying the antecedent is another one of the three formal fallacies. This fallacy is also known as inverse error, or fallacy of the inverse. This logical fallacy occurs where someone assumes that if the first part of the antecedent is false, then the consequent must also be false.

Non Sequitur

Non Sequitur is the third and last formal fallacy. This fallacy is a statement or conclusion that does not logically follow the previous statement, or the conversation at all. This term comes from Latin and means, quite literally, "It does not follow"

Informal fallacies of relevance

Ad Hominem

The ad hominem fallacy is a logical fallacy that happens when someone attacks the person making the argument instead of the argument itself. The term comes from Latin and mean "to the person". This attack can also be direct to a membership in a group or institution.

Appeal to authority

The appeal to authority fallacy happens when someone claims something is true only because another person said so, without providing any evidence to support their claim. This fallacy makes the person rely on the person's status instead of the argument and the arguments he is making against the other person.

Appeal to authority example:

Appeal to Emotion

Appeal to emotion, is also known as the argumentum ad passiones or argument from passion. This is a fallacy that involves manipulating someone's emotions to win an argument, especially when there is no actual evidence. It is problematic tactic because it creates a weak argument, bypasses people's logic and critical thinking, and is manipulative.

Appeal to fear

Appeal to fear is also called argumentum ad metum or argumentum in terrorem. This fallacy is when a person tries to create support for an idea by applying the fear to the other option. It is a common tactic in marketing, politics, and media.

Appeal to Ignorance

Appeal to Ignorance is the fallacy when you argue your conclusion must be true, only because there is no evidence against it. This fallacy wrongly shifts the burden of proof away from the one making the claim.

Appeal to Ignorance Example:

Appeal to Tradition

An appeal to tradition is the logical fallacy that claims something is correct because it has been done in the past or is a part of a tradition. It means to ignore the evidence that we should change becuase we have been doing something for a long time. They are particularly dangerous when they prevent change.

Bandwagon Fallacy

The Bandwagon Fallacy, also known as the appeal to popularity, appeal to the masses, or appeal to common belief,is a logical fallacy that occurs when someone argues that something is correct because a majority of people support it. It mostly happens when one person wants to fit in.

Red Herring

The Red Herring fallacy occurs when a misleading argument or question is used to change the topic of the argument, and divert attention from the main issue. The term comes from British fox hunters dragging a smelly red herring across a trail to throw dogs off their scent.

Straw Man

The straw man fallacy is a logical fallacy that occurs when someone misrepresents an opponent's argument to make it easier to attack. The person using this fallacy weakens the opponent's argument by oversimplifying or exaggerating it, and then elaborating on the new version. The person using the straw man fallacy pretents to argue against the opponent's original position, but creates an easier version of the arguement to tackcle.

Informal fallacies of ambiguity

Equivocation

The equivocation fallacy occurs when a key term or phrase in an argument is used in an ambiguous way, with one meaning in one portion of the argument and one in the other portion of the argument. This Fallacy causes confusion and makes the conclusion not flow with the argument.

Amphiboly

Amphiboly is a common logical fallacy that happens when a statement's grammar is ambiguous, leading to multiple different interpretation. It can lead to confusion and misunderstandings, and can hinder personal growth. This fallacy can also lead to very serious consequences.
